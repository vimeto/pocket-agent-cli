\title{Where Should LLM Agents Run? Measuring On-Device, Edge, and Cloud Deployment Costs}

\begin{abstract}
LLM-powered agents are rapidly moving to mobile and edge devices, yet no systematic study quantifies where inference should run for iterative, tool-calling workloads.
We present Pocket Agent, a measurement framework that instruments complete agentic loops (Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation cycles) across three deployment architectures (local, hybrid, cloud), seven models (0.6B--4B parameters), three hardware platforms (smartphone, laptop, GPU server), and four benchmarks (MBPP, HumanEval, BFCL, Web Search QA).
Our evaluation across seven network conditions yields three findings.
First, agentic workloads are compute-dominated: even at 500\,ms RTT, network overhead adds less than 4\% to end-to-end latency, making the deployment decision largely network-agnostic for current model sizes.
Second, thinking models waste both compute and radio resources: an early-exit thinking budget improves accuracy by 6--10 percentage points while reducing energy by 39--84\%, and 95\% of inter-request gaps exceed cellular RRC timers, wasting 95\% of radio energy on tail states.
Third, context compression exhibits a three-way tradeoff between bandwidth savings, accuracy, and prefix-cache efficiency: stripping thinking tokens from context improves accuracy by 6.6pp while heavier compression degrades both accuracy and cache efficiency.
We characterize agentic inference as a new mobile traffic class and evaluate six placement policies, finding that a simple cost-aware rule achieves 97.5\% of oracle performance while reducing energy by 62\% versus always-local execution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) are transitioning from cloud-hosted query-response systems into interactive agents that invoke tools, plan actions, and process observations iteratively~\cite{li-2025-review,10606356}.
On-device deployment of such agents promises privacy, low latency, and offline availability~\cite{8876870}, and commercial systems already ship small models on phones and laptops.
Yet a fundamental deployment question remains open: \emph{where should the inference run?}
Running locally preserves privacy (tool operations touch local files, personal data, and on-device APIs that never leave the device) but is constrained by memory, thermals, and throughput.
Offloading to a cloud server provides abundant compute but exposes prompts and tool outputs to the network, introduces latency, and incurs radio energy costs that scale with tool-calling round-trips.
A hybrid architecture, where cloud inference pairs with on-device tool execution, may balance privacy with performance, but no prior work has quantified these tradeoffs for the iterative, multi-turn workloads that characterize modern agents.

Unlike single-shot inference, agentic usage introduces iterative Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation loops that amplify latency, memory, and energy consumption.
When solving a coding problem, the model selects a code execution tool (Tool), runs a test (Action), receives feedback (Observation), and iterates with corrections.
Each iteration compounds costs: context grows, prefill stages lengthen, and, for hybrid or cloud deployments, every round-trip triggers cellular radio state transitions that consume energy long after transmission ends.
Chain-of-thought models exacerbate this by generating thousands of reasoning tokens that never reach the user, wasting both compute and, when offloaded, network bandwidth.

This paper addresses three research questions:

\noindent\textbf{Q1:} What is the true cost of agentic loops across local, hybrid, and cloud deployment architectures?

\noindent\textbf{Q2:} How do network conditions (RTT, bandwidth, radio state) affect the deployment decision?

\noindent\textbf{Q3:} Can we reduce agentic cost through thinking budgets and context compression without sacrificing accuracy?

\medskip
\noindent We make the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Pocket Agent framework.} A multi-engine (MLX, SGLang, vLLM), multi-platform measurement framework that instruments complete agentic loops with network simulation, thinking budget control, and unified energy monitoring.

\item \textbf{Three-architecture comparison.} We evaluate local, hybrid, and cloud deployment across seven RTT conditions and five models, finding that compute dominance makes the deployment decision largely network-agnostic: network adds less than 4\% overhead even at 500\,ms RTT.

\item \textbf{Early-exit thinking budget.} Capping reasoning tokens at 1024--2048 improves accuracy by 6--10 percentage points and reduces energy by 39--84\%, establishing a Pareto frontier for thinking investment.

\item \textbf{Agentic traffic characterization.} We identify agentic inference as a new mobile traffic class with median 10.2\,s inter-request gaps, where 95\% of radio energy is wasted on tail states and RRC timer tuning cannot address the structural mismatch.

\item \textbf{Context compression tradeoff.} We quantify a three-way tradeoff between bandwidth savings, accuracy, and prefix-cache efficiency, finding that moderate compression (stripping thinking tokens) improves accuracy by 6.6pp while aggressive compression degrades both accuracy ($-$10pp) and cache hit rates.

\item \textbf{Broad evaluation.} Seven models (including Qwen~3.5 and Gemma~4 for generalizability), four benchmarks (MBPP, HumanEval, BFCL, Web Search QA), and five hardware platforms spanning mobile (iPhone~15, iPhone~16 Pro, OnePlus 10 Pro), laptop (MacBook M2 Max), and GPU server (NVIDIA A100).
\end{enumerate}

Pocket Agent is released at \url{https://github.com/[anonymized]}, including all code, configurations, and analysis scripts.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{LLM Inference Systems and Runtimes.}
On-device inference engines span hand-tuned libraries such as \texttt{llama.cpp}~\cite{llamacpp} and MLX for Apple Silicon, and compiler-based frameworks like MLC-LLM~\cite{mlc-llm} that prioritize hardware portability.
Server-side systems including SGLang and vLLM exploit continuous batching, PagedAttention, and RadixAttention to maximize GPU utilization.
Recent works exploit activation locality (PowerInfer-2~\cite{powerinfer2}) or target mobile NPUs directly~\cite{llm-npu2024}.
All prior systems evaluate single-pass prompt $\rightarrow$ response generation rather than the iterative tool-calling workflows that characterize modern agents.

\textbf{Model Compression.}
Weight-only post-training quantization remains the dominant approach for deploying multi-billion parameter models on memory-constrained devices.
AWQ~\cite{awq2023} preserves quality at 4-bit precision, the GGUF format~\cite{gguf2024} standardizes block-wise quantization, and MobileQuant~\cite{mobilequant2025} pushes activation precision lower for edge deployment.
We previously showed that quantization benefits are architecture-dependent: models without native INT4 GPU kernels can regress 40$\times$ in throughput on Apple Silicon.

\textbf{LLM Offloading and Split Inference.}
DRAGON~\cite{dragon2025} proposes disaggregated inference for resource-constrained devices at MobiHoc'25, partitioning prefill and decode across edge and cloud.
Splitwise~\cite{splitwise2024} separates prefill and decode phases across GPU clusters.
EdgeShard~\cite{edgeshard2024} partitions models across heterogeneous edge devices via collaborative inference.
These systems target single-request latency; we complement them by characterizing multi-turn agentic workloads where context growth and tool-calling patterns create distinct traffic and energy profiles.

\textbf{Efficient Reasoning.}
Chain-of-thought models devote the vast majority of output tokens to reasoning.
Budget forcing~\cite{budgetforcing2025} constrains thinking length during training;
NOWAIT~\cite{nowait2025} proposes speculative execution of reasoning steps;
``Don't Overthink It''~\cite{dontoverthink2025} shows that shorter traces often yield better results.
We provide empirical evidence for this in the deployment context: our early-exit mechanism improves both accuracy and energy efficiency by terminating unproductive deliberation.

\textbf{Measurement and Characterization.}
MLPerf Inference~\cite{mlperf-inference2025} formalizes latency targets for single-pass inference.
Mobile profiling studies~\cite{xiao2025understandinglargelanguagemodels,llm-npu2024} reveal that mobile GPUs operate at under 20\% utilization and prefill dominates long-context latency.
Unlike prior work that measures prompt $\rightarrow$ response in isolation, Pocket Agent quantifies complete agentic loops across deployment architectures, including network and radio costs.

\section{Preliminaries and Metrics}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

\subsection{Agentic Execution Modes}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Execution modes for measuring LLM agents.}
\label{tab:modes}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{} l l l @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{Tools Available} & \textbf{Purpose} \\
\midrule
Base & None & Baseline: direct submission \\
Tool-Submission & Submission tool only & Structured output overhead \\
Full-Tool & Model-selected tools & Complete agentic workflow \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We evaluate agents in three modes (Table~\ref{tab:modes}).
\emph{Base} mode measures intrinsic capability via direct code generation.
\emph{Tool-Submission} mode requires structured output via a submission tool~\cite{openai2023functioncalling}, isolating the overhead of structured generation.
\emph{Full-Tool} mode enables iterative tool invocation (code execution, file operations), capturing the ReAct~\cite{yao2022react} pattern of multi-turn Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation loops.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

\textbf{Latency.}
TTFT (time-to-first-token) captures prefill overhead; End-to-End Time includes all tool execution and subsequent LLM calls.

\textbf{Throughput.}
We distinguish \emph{completion TPS} (tokens generated per second for a single request, determining user-perceived latency) from \emph{server throughput} (aggregate tokens per second across concurrent requests).
On-device inference (MLX, llama.cpp) is single-request; server inference via SGLang processes batches of up to 150 concurrent requests using continuous batching.
All per-model TPS values in this paper refer to completion TPS unless stated otherwise.

\textbf{Success.}
Pass@k is the probability a correct solution appears within $k$ attempts~\cite{chen2021evaluating}. We report Pass@1 with temperature 0.7.

\textbf{Energy.}
Energy per Token~= $\mathrm{Power}_{\mathrm{gen}} / \mathrm{TPS}_{\mathrm{completion}}$.
\emph{Energy per Successful Solution} sums energy across all attempts until success: $E_{\text{succ}} = \sum_{a=1}^{k^*} E_a$, where $k^*$ is the first successful attempt~\cite{schwartz2020greenai}, capturing the true cost of retries.
Our energy measurements use single-user completion TPS, representing the per-user cost; under batch utilization, server energy per token is amortized across concurrent users (Section~\ref{sec:costmodel}).

\subsection{Deployment Architectures}

We evaluate three deployment architectures:

\textbf{Local.} All inference and tool execution run on the device. No network traffic; full privacy preservation. Latency is bounded by device throughput.

\textbf{Cloud.} All inference and tool execution are offloaded to a remote server. Best throughput, but prompts, tool outputs, and code traverse the network, exposing private data. Radio energy is consumed for every round-trip.

\textbf{Hybrid.} Inference runs in the cloud; tools execute on-device. This mirrors commercial deployments where the LLM reasons remotely but tool operations (file access, code execution, local data queries) stay on the device for privacy. Each agentic iteration requires a network round-trip, creating the traffic patterns analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:traffic}.

\subsection{The Agentic Loop}

Unlike single-shot inference, agentic execution follows an iterative loop~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine}.
The model receives a task and generates a response that may include tool invocations (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{1}).
The system executes the requested tools (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{2}) and returns observations (e.g., test results or error messages).
The model processes this feedback (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{3}) and may issue further tool calls (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{4}) or submit a final solution.
Context grows with each iteration: memory pressure accumulates, and for cloud or hybrid deployments each round-trip incurs network latency and radio energy costs.

\subsection{Multi-Engine Support}

To evaluate deployment architectures fairly, Pocket Agent supports multiple inference backends spanning mobile, laptop, and server platforms:

\textbf{llama.cpp} targets mobile devices (iPhone, Android) via Metal (iOS) and CPU/Vulkan (Android) backends. Models use Q4\_K\_M quantization to fit within 6--8\,GB RAM constraints. Tool execution uses Pyodide WebAssembly sandboxes on mobile (Docker on desktop).

\textbf{MLX} targets Apple Silicon (MacBook M2 Max in our evaluation). MLX exploits unified memory and Metal GPU kernels for efficient on-device inference with FP16 and INT4 precision.

\textbf{SGLang} targets NVIDIA GPUs (A100 in our evaluation). SGLang uses RadixAttention for automatic prefix caching, continuous batching, and optimized CUDA kernels. We use SGLang for all cloud-architecture experiments.

\textbf{vLLM} provides an alternative server backend with PagedAttention. We use vLLM for Gemma~4, which requires its custom attention implementation.

All engines expose a unified API for the agentic loop, enabling controlled comparisons across architectures.

\subsection{Network Simulation Layer}

To evaluate hybrid and cloud deployments under controlled conditions, Pocket Agent includes a network simulation layer with seven RTT presets: local (0\,ms), WiFi-fast (5\,ms), WiFi (20\,ms), 5G (50\,ms), 4G (100\,ms), congested (200\,ms), and satellite (500\,ms).

The simulator wraps each API call with calibrated delay injection and bandwidth throttling.
For radio energy estimation, we implement a 3GPP-based RRC state machine~\cite{huang2012close_look_power} that models Connected $\rightarrow$ Short DRX $\rightarrow$ Long DRX $\rightarrow$ Idle transitions with configurable inactivity timers (default: 7\,s tail timer, following typical LTE/5G operator configurations).
The hybrid wrapper additionally models local prefill followed by cloud decode, with network overhead applied only to the offloaded portion.

\subsection{Thinking Budget Mechanism}

Chain-of-thought models emit reasoning tokens in \texttt{<think>} tags before producing actionable output.
We show that thinking-token volume correlates \emph{negatively} with task success: the model thinks longer because it is stuck, not because extended reasoning helps (Section~\ref{sec:thinking}).

Pocket Agent implements an early-exit mechanism via two-phase generation.
In the first phase, the model generates normally until either the \texttt{</think>} tag appears or a configurable token budget $B$ is reached.
If the budget is exceeded, the system injects a \texttt{</think>} token into the context and begins the second phase, which generates only actionable output.
This mechanism requires no model modification; it operates at the inference-engine level by manipulating the token stream.

\subsection{Monitoring}

Pocket Agent records per-token timestamps, power draw (macOS \texttt{powermetrics}, NVML on GPUs, battery APIs on mobile), thermal state, memory, and network metrics (payloads, RTTs, radio state).
Measurements are logged at 10\,Hz and synchronized.
On mobile, thermal-aware scheduling pauses at safe thresholds (50\textdegree{}C phones, 65\textdegree{}C laptops) to prevent throttling during sustained agentic loops.

\textbf{iPhone 16 Pro} (A18 Pro, 8\,GB RAM): secondary mobile platform used for throughput comparison, demonstrating generational improvements in mobile inference (2.6$\times$ vs.\ OnePlus 10 Pro). Uses \texttt{llama.cpp} with Q4\_K\_M quantization.

\textbf{OnePlus 10 Pro} (Snapdragon 8 Gen 1, 12\,GB RAM): Android reference point using \texttt{llama.cpp} with Q4\_K\_M quantization. Provides the slowest throughput in our device matrix (4.9~TPS) and the highest thermal sensitivity (31\% degradation after 90\,s without thermal management).

\textbf{NVIDIA A100} (40\,GB HBM2e, 256\,GB system RAM): cloud/edge server using SGLang (or vLLM for Gemma~4) with FP16 precision and prefix caching enabled.

\subsection{Benchmarks}

\textbf{MBPP}~\cite{austin2021programsynthesislargelanguage}: 500 Python problems (IDs 10--509) with 3--5 test cases each. Our primary benchmark for all experiments.

\textbf{HumanEval}~\cite{chen2021evaluating}: 164 hand-crafted problems with detailed docstrings and 5--10 test cases. Used for cross-dataset validation.

\textbf{BFCL} (Berkeley Function-Calling Leaderboard): 45 function-calling tasks that isolate tool-calling proficiency from coding ability, testing argument extraction, multi-tool selection, and schema adherence.

\textbf{Web Search QA}: 100 questions requiring web-search tool invocation and answer synthesis, evaluating agents on information retrieval tasks outside code generation.

\subsection{Protocol}

All experiments use temperature 0.7, top-$p$ 0.95, and Pass@1 evaluation.
A100 experiments run 10 samples per task on full datasets (500 MBPP, 164 HumanEval).
MacBook experiments run the full MBPP dataset with 10 attempts.
Network experiments use the simulation layer with controlled RTT and bandwidth.
We interpret results comparatively (between modes, models, and architectures) rather than as absolute numbers, given likely training data contamination in code benchmarks.

\section{On-Device Agent Characterization}
\label{sec:ondevice}

Before evaluating deployment architectures, we characterize agent behavior on its own terms: capability baselines, thinking-token dynamics, and the early-exit mechanism that underpins our deployment analysis.

\subsection{Capability Threshold and Error Analysis}
\label{sec:capability}
\label{sec:errors}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/new/fig1_pass_at_1_comparison.pdf}
  \caption{Pass@1 across seven models, three execution modes, and two benchmarks. The left panel (MBPP, 500 problems) and right panel (HumanEval, 164 problems) each show three bars per model---sage green (Base), salmon (Tool-Submission), and steel blue (Full-Tool)---revealing the progression from no tools to full agentic tool use. Qwen~3 (4B) gains substantially from tools (+9pp on MBPP, +7pp on HumanEval), while Llama~3.2 and DeepSeek R1 degrade, revealing a capability threshold for effective tool use.}
  \label{fig:pass1}
  \Description{Bar chart with left panel (MBPP) and right panel (HumanEval), each showing three bars per model in sage green (Base), salmon (Tool-Submission), and steel blue (Full-Tool). Qwen 3 4B gains substantially from tools while Llama and DeepSeek are hurt.}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:pass1} presents Pass@1 across all seven models and three execution modes on MBPP (left panel, 500 problems) and HumanEval (right panel, 164 problems), indicating a clear capability threshold for effective tool use. For each model group, the three colored bars---sage green (Base), salmon (Tool-Submission), and steel blue (Full-Tool)---show the progression from no tools to full agentic tool use. On MBPP with cloud FP16 inference, the leftmost model group (Qwen~3 4B) shows a clear upward trend: the sage green bar sits at 68\%, the salmon bar rises to 79\%, and the steel blue bar reaches 77\%, a net +9pp gain from structured tool use. In contrast, models below the threshold are actively \emph{harmed} by tools: the Llama~3.2 group shows the steel blue bar dropping below the sage green bar (50\% to 43\%, $-$7pp), and DeepSeek R1 shows a similar decline (42\% to 33\%, $-$9pp) in Full-Tool mode, as these models generate malformed tool calls or invoke tools at inappropriate times. Gemma~3n illustrates an intermediate case: its salmon bar matches the sage green bar (61\%) but the steel blue bar drops sharply to 35\%, indicating that it can produce structured output but cannot sustain multi-turn tool use. Qwen~3 (0.6B) degrades from 42\% (Base) to 34\% in both Tool-Submission and Full-Tool modes, consistent with insufficient capability at this scale.

We validated these findings on HumanEval using identical model configurations. The capability threshold pattern holds: Qwen~3 (4B) rises from 73\% (Base) to 80\% in both Tool-Submission and Full-Tool modes (+7pp), consistent with the threshold being dataset-independent. The consistent result across both benchmarks (Qwen benefits while Llama degrades) provides strong evidence that the capability threshold is a fundamental property of the model rather than a dataset-specific artifact. On the MacBook via MLX, Qwen~3 (4B) achieves its +9pp tool benefit with no additional latency cost (77\,s Full-Tool vs.\ 81\,s Base), because the iterative tool-calling loop produces better solutions in fewer total tokens than unconstrained single-pass generation.

To test generalizability beyond our core model set, we evaluated Qwen~3.5 (4B) and Gemma~4 (4B) on a 50-problem MBPP subset with the same protocol (the two rightmost model groups in Figure~\ref{fig:pass1}, shown as solid bars for original models and hatched bars for the newer probes). Qwen~3.5 achieves 70\%/78\%/86\% (Base/Tool-Submission/Full-Tool), demonstrating that newer models amplify the tool benefit (+16pp, nearly twice the gain of Qwen~3 after eleven months of development). Gemma~4 shows 72\%/70\%/72\%, maintaining but not improving with tools, capable enough to avoid degradation but unable to leverage iterative refinement. On HumanEval, the gap widens: Qwen~3.5 reaches 76\%/85\%/91\% (+15pp), while Gemma~4 drops to 79\%/59\%/57\% ($-$22pp), indicating that tool-calling proficiency varies even among models of similar size.

To isolate tool-calling ability from coding capability, we additionally evaluate on BFCL (Figure~\ref{fig:bfcl}). In the stacked bar chart, green segments (full match) dominate for capable models while rose segments (no match) dominate for weaker ones: Qwen~3.5 is entirely green at 100\% accuracy, Gemma~3n and Gemma~4 each reach 98\%, and Qwen~3 (4B) scores 93\%, while DeepSeek R1's bar is mostly rose at only 49\%. This confirms that DeepSeek's poor Full-Tool performance stems from tool-calling deficiency rather than reasoning weakness, and explains why Gemma~3n (98\% BFCL, 35\% MBPP Full-Tool) can parse tool schemas but cannot sustain productive multi-turn tool use. Looking across the twenty-month span of our model releases (Table~\ref{tab:models}), a clear chronological trend emerges: tool-calling capability is improving faster than base coding ability. Qwen~3 (Apr '25) gains +9pp from tools on MBPP; Qwen~3.5 (Mar '26) gains +16pp at similar base accuracy. DeepSeek R1 (Jan '25) remains an outlier: strong reasoning but 49\% BFCL, showing that thinking capability and tool-calling evolved on different timelines. This trajectory suggests the capability threshold for effective tool use is dropping, and deployment systems should anticipate tool augmentation becoming beneficial for progressively smaller models.

To understand \emph{why} models fail, we classified 80{,}000 failure cases across MBPP and HumanEval into three categories: incorrect logic, fixable errors (syntax, missing imports), and runtime exceptions. HumanEval exhibits nearly 2$\times$ the rate of fixable errors compared to MBPP (37\% vs.\ 19\%), predominantly missing \texttt{typing} imports (e.g., \texttt{List[int]}, \texttt{Dict[str, Any]}) triggered by docstring type annotations, while MBPP shows more fundamental logic failures (45\% vs.\ 35\%), reflecting its emphasis on algorithmic reasoning over API familiarity. Models that benefit from tools also iterate more effectively: Qwen~3 (4B) averages 3.11 iterations with a 57\% submission rate, indicating deliberate refinement before committing, while Gemma~3n averages only 1.27 iterations with 94\% submission rate, suggesting premature commitment. The correlation between iteration depth and tool benefit (Pearson $r{=}0.72$) suggests that effective tool use requires sustained multi-turn reasoning capacity, not just syntactically correct tool invocations.

\subsection{Cross-Platform Throughput, Power, and Prefill Latency}
\label{sec:crossplatform}
\label{sec:prefill}

\subsection{Energy Model, Quantization, and Thermals}
\label{sec:costmodel}
\label{sec:quantization}

We validate a simple energy model, $E = P \times N / \text{TPS}_{\text{completion}}$, where $P$ is mean generation power, $N$ is total tokens generated, and $\text{TPS}_{\text{completion}}$ is single-request decode throughput. This model achieves $R^2=0.89$ across all model-platform combinations, confirming that token count and throughput are sufficient predictors of energy consumption. Two findings emerge. First, thinking models consume $3.7\times$ more energy than non-thinking models of comparable size, driven by $3$--$5\times$ more tokens per attempt rather than higher per-token cost. Second, the model exposes an \emph{inverse efficiency paradox} that spans three orders of magnitude: Qwen~3 (4B) consumes 0.1\,kJ (A100), 1.2\,kJ (MacBook), and 10.3\,kJ (iPhone~15) per successful solution in Full-Tool mode. This counterintuitive result arises because mobile devices take so much longer to complete tasks (iPhone~15 uses $5$--$8\times$ more energy than the MacBook and $50$--$100\times$ more than A100, despite drawing far less instantaneous power) that total energy consumption far exceeds that of more powerful platforms. Energy per successful solution is ultimately dominated by two factors: token count (thinking overhead) and first-pass success rate (retry cost). Figure~\ref{fig:energy} visualizes this paradox: in the grouped bar chart, each model's three bars (blue for A100, green for MacBook M2 Max, salmon for iPhone~15) show the per-platform energy on a log-scale y-axis. The visual gap between the leftmost bar (A100) and rightmost bar (iPhone~15) spans two orders of magnitude for every model, making the 50--100$\times$ mobile penalty immediately apparent.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/new/fig_energy_per_success.pdf}
  \caption{Energy per successful solution across three platforms (A100, MacBook M2 Max, iPhone~15) for all five core models in Full-Tool mode with Q4\_K\_M quantization. The inverse efficiency paradox spans three orders of magnitude: iPhone~15 consumes 50--100$\times$ more energy than A100 despite drawing far less instantaneous power, because lower TPS extends generation time.}
  \label{fig:energy}
  \Description{Grouped bar chart showing energy per successful solution for five models across A100, MacBook, and iPhone 15, with log-scale y-axis showing the 100x gap.}
\end{figure}

Under production batch utilization, the cloud's advantage widens. SGLang with continuous batching at 150 concurrent requests drives the A100 GPU to $\approx$280\,W (full-node draw including CPU and memory would be $\approx$550\,W), but amortized across all users, the per-token GPU energy drops to $\approx$0.03\,J/token, roughly $35\times$ lower than single-request operation. Even accounting for full-node overhead, the amortized per-user energy remains orders of magnitude below mobile. Our single-user measurements therefore represent a conservative estimate of the cloud's energy advantage.

For capable models, tool augmentation also reduces total energy cost. Qwen~3 (4B) drops from 3.09\,kJ to 1.18\,kJ per successful solution on the MacBook (62\% reduction) and from 0.138\,kJ to 0.105\,kJ on A100 (24\%), because higher Pass@1 eliminates costly retries. However, this benefit is model-dependent: for models below the capability threshold, tools increase energy by adding failed iterations without improving success rate. This finding inverts the conventional optimization target: for on-device agents, maximizing first-pass success rate is more important than minimizing per-token inference cost, because retry energy dominates the total budget.

Quantization interacts with these energy dynamics in a runtime-dependent way. In our prior study using \texttt{llama.cpp}, Gemma~3n at Q4\_K\_M regressed ${\approx}40\times$ in throughput on the MacBook, from 24.4 to 0.6~TPS, because \texttt{llama.cpp} lacks Metal kernels for Gemma's sliding-window attention, forcing CPU fallback. MLX eliminates this regression entirely, which is why we use it for the architecture comparison in Section~\ref{sec:network} and why Gemma~3n is the sole model where local outperforms cloud. On A100, Q4\_K\_M vs.\ FP16 differences are within 5--10\%, as CUDA provides native INT4 kernels for all architectures. Quantization decisions must be validated against the target runtime, not assumed to transfer across engines. Beyond throughput, quantization also affects accuracy. Comparing SGLang FP16 with MLX Q4\_K\_M on MBPP Base mode, Qwen~3 (0.6B) loses 18.4pp under INT4 quantization, while larger models are less sensitive: Qwen~3 (4B) loses 3.5pp, Gemma~3n 3.3pp, and Llama~3.2 6.5pp. DeepSeek R1 is essentially unaffected ($-$3.3pp). The smallest models are thus most vulnerable to quantization, compounding their already limited capability.

Finally, thermal throttling poses a practical constraint on sustained mobile inference. Without thermal management, OnePlus 10 Pro degrades 31\% in throughput after 90 seconds of sustained inference, while iPhones maintain stability for approximately 180 seconds before throttling. Our thermal-aware scheduling pauses at safe temperature thresholds (50\textdegree{}C for phones, 65\textdegree{}C for laptops) to prevent throttling during extended agentic loops, but the need for such pauses indicates that mobile agentic workloads approach the thermal limits of current hardware.

Extrapolating from our energy model and the per-platform data in Tables~\ref{tab:power} and~\ref{tab:platform-tps}, the inverse efficiency paradox is most pronounced on smartphones. Qwen~3 (4B) consumes 10.3\,kJ per successful solution on iPhone~15 in Full-Tool mode versus 0.1\,kJ on A100, a $100\times$ gap that far exceeds the laptop-to-server ratio ($12\times$). Gemma~3n's MLX-based local advantage on the MacBook does not exist on iOS, where inference uses \texttt{llama.cpp} without architecture-specific optimizations. For current mobile hardware, cloud offloading is the dominant strategy for all models.

The overarching finding is that network overhead is negligible. Across all models and RTT conditions, it adds less than 4\% to end-to-end latency. The reason is structural: agentic payloads are small (2--21\,KB per request) and tool-calling rounds are few (3--5), while inference time ranges from 3 to 63 seconds, yielding a network-to-compute ratio on the order of $10^{-2}$ even under adversarial conditions. Pass@1 is consistent across platforms (MLX and SGLang yield comparable accuracy for all models). To test whether bandwidth constraints might shift this picture, we swept bandwidth from 0.5 to 100\,Mbps across three RTT conditions and found no crossover point at any realistic bandwidth. At 0.5\,Mbps, below typical 4G throughput, cloud latency for Qwen~3 (4B) increases by only 0.3\,s, because payload sizes are small (median 8.4\,KB per request, 14.2\,KB per response); even the largest payloads (21\,KB) transfer in under 0.4\,s. The compute-to-transfer ratio exceeds 100:1 at all tested bandwidths. For current model sizes and agentic workloads, neither latency nor bandwidth constrains the deployment decision; it should instead be driven by compute availability, energy cost, and privacy requirements. A simple two-signal heuristic suffices: offload to the cloud when cloud TPS exceeds local TPS by more than $2\times$ and model size exceeds 1B parameters; otherwise, run locally. This rule routes Gemma~3n locally while offloading all other models, reducing energy by 62\% versus always-local execution.

For hybrid and cloud deployments, each tool-calling round-trip triggers radio state transitions. Inference time (3--63\,s) vastly exceeds any practical timer setting: increasing the timer to match inference time would waste energy for \emph{every} mobile application, while decreasing it further would increase re-establishment overhead. The problem requires application-layer solutions (such as performing local inference for thinking tokens and offloading only actionable output to the cloud) rather than radio-layer tuning.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our measurements span seven models, four benchmarks, five hardware platforms, and seven network conditions. We return to the three questions posed in Section~\ref{sec:introduction} and synthesize our findings.

The first question---\emph{what is the true cost of agentic loops across deployment architectures?}---is answered by the data: agentic workloads are compute-dominated. Cloud deployment via SGLang provides 2--3$\times$ lower latency than local MLX inference for most models, and network overhead adds less than 4\% even at 500\,ms RTT. The sole exception is Gemma~3n, where architecture-specific local optimizations outperform the cloud, illustrating that the deployment decision hinges on the match between model architecture and inference engine. Thinking models consume 3.7$\times$ more energy than non-thinking models of comparable size, driven by token volume rather than per-token cost, and an inverse efficiency paradox makes mobile devices the \emph{least} energy-efficient platform despite drawing the least instantaneous power.

The second question---\emph{how do network conditions affect the deployment decision?}---yields a concise answer: they largely do not, at least for latency and throughput. Network conditions have minimal impact on end-to-end latency (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}), and bandwidth is never the bottleneck even at 0.5\,Mbps. The primary network cost is \emph{energetic}: agentic traffic produces bursty, low-volume transmissions with multi-second inter-request gaps that systematically exceed RRC timers, wasting 95\% of radio energy on tail states (Section~\ref{sec:traffic}). A two-signal placement heuristic (TPS ratio and model size) captures this cost structure, reducing energy by 62\% versus always-local execution (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}).

The third question---\emph{can we reduce cost through thinking budgets and context compression?}---has an affirmative answer with practical implications. Early-exit thinking budgets at 1024--2048 tokens improve accuracy by 6--10pp and reduce energy by 39--84\% for capable thinking models (Section~\ref{sec:earlyexit}), while moderate context compression (stripping thinking tokens) improves accuracy by 6.6pp by removing distracting reasoning traces from the context (Section~\ref{sec:compression}). The two mechanisms are complementary: thinking budgets reduce compute cost at generation time, while compression curates the context for subsequent iterations.

Based on these findings, we distill actionable guidelines across four dimensions. For \emph{deployment}, cloud offloading is preferred for models above 1B without architecture-specific local advantages, as compute savings dominate network costs; the cost-aware placement rule (TPS ratio + model size) suffices, and bandwidth monitoring adds no value. In practice, applications should implement capability-gated agency: begin in Base mode and escalate to Full-Tool only when model capability and task complexity warrant it. For \emph{inference}, thinking budgets should be enabled by default for thinking models ($B{=}2048$ as a conservative starting point, $B{=}1024$ for efficient reasoners), and context compression at L2 (strip thinking tokens) is the default operating point for cloud deployments. For \emph{networking}, agentic workloads constitute a new traffic class poorly served by current radio resource management; application-layer solutions (batching outputs, local reasoning with cloud-offloaded action, or predictive wake-up) can bridge the mismatch between inference timing and RRC timers. For \emph{privacy}, the hybrid architecture preserves tool-level privacy while capturing most of the cloud's throughput advantage, and context compression further reduces information exposure by stripping verbose tool outputs before transmission.

Our evaluation covers code-generation and QA tasks; extending to multi-modal agents and longer-horizon planning would broaden applicability. Network conditions are simulated rather than measured on real cellular networks, and the RRC energy model uses standard 3GPP parameters that may differ from operator-specific configurations. Mobile phone measurements cover throughput, power, and prefill latency but not the full three-architecture comparison, which requires SGLang/vLLM that currently lack mobile ports. Mobile energy values carry $\pm$15\% uncertainty due to indirect measurement via battery and thermal APIs. Three directions follow naturally from our findings: deploying the hybrid architecture on real cellular networks with application-layer optimizations (batching tool outputs, preferring WiFi, deferring non-urgent agentic tasks) to reduce the radio energy waste; extending to multi-agent coordination scenarios where models on different devices coordinate, multiplying both compute and network costs; and co-designing radio resource management policies with inference scheduling to address the structural mismatch between agentic traffic patterns and RRC state machines.

\begin{insightbox}{Two signals suffice for placement}
For current agentic workloads with models up to 4B parameters, the deployment decision is driven by compute availability and energy cost, not network conditions.
A two-signal placement rule (TPS ratio, model size) achieves near-oracle performance.
The networking challenge is not latency or bandwidth but radio energy waste from timing mismatches between inference patterns and cellular RRC state machines.
\end{insightbox}
