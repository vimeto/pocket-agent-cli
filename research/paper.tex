\title{Where Should LLM Agents Run? Measuring On-Device, Edge, and Cloud Deployment Costs}

\begin{abstract}
LLM-powered agents are rapidly moving to mobile and edge devices, yet no systematic study quantifies where inference should run for iterative, tool-calling workloads.
We present Pocket Agent, a measurement framework that instruments complete agentic loops---Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation cycles---across three deployment architectures (local, hybrid, cloud), seven models (0.6B--4B parameters), and four benchmarks (MBPP, HumanEval, BFCL, Web Search QA).
Our evaluation across seven network conditions reveals three findings.
First, agentic workloads are compute-dominated: even at 500\,ms RTT, network overhead adds less than 4\% to end-to-end latency, making the deployment decision largely network-agnostic for current model sizes.
Second, thinking models waste both compute and radio resources---an early-exit thinking budget improves accuracy by 6--10 percentage points while reducing energy by 39--84\%, and 95\% of inter-request gaps exceed cellular RRC timers, wasting 95\% of radio energy on tail states.
Third, context compression exhibits a three-way tradeoff between bandwidth savings, accuracy, and prefix-cache efficiency, with a sweet spot at moderate compression (+4pp accuracy, $-$40\% bandwidth).
We characterize agentic inference as a new mobile traffic class and evaluate six placement policies, finding that a simple cost-aware rule achieves 97.5\% of oracle performance while reducing energy by 62\% versus always-local execution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) are transitioning from cloud-hosted query-response systems into interactive agents that invoke tools, plan actions, and process observations iteratively~\cite{li-2025-review,10606356}.
On-device deployment of such agents promises privacy, low latency, and offline availability~\cite{8876870}, and commercial systems already ship small models on phones and laptops.
Yet a fundamental deployment question remains open: \emph{where should the inference run?}
Running locally avoids network costs but is constrained by memory, thermals, and throughput.
Offloading to a cloud server provides abundant compute but introduces network latency, bandwidth consumption, and radio energy costs that scale with the number of tool-calling round-trips.
A hybrid architecture---local prefill with cloud decode, or selective offloading---may combine the best of both, but no prior work has quantified these tradeoffs for the iterative, multi-turn workloads that characterize modern agents.

Unlike single-shot inference, agentic usage introduces iterative Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation loops that amplify latency, memory, and energy consumption.
When solving a coding problem, the model selects a code execution tool (Tool), runs a test (Action), receives feedback (Observation), and iterates with corrections.
Each iteration compounds costs: context grows, prefill stages lengthen, and---for hybrid or cloud deployments---every round-trip triggers cellular radio state transitions that consume energy long after transmission ends.
Chain-of-thought models exacerbate this by generating thousands of reasoning tokens that never reach the user, wasting both compute and, when offloaded, network bandwidth.

This paper addresses three research questions:

\noindent\textbf{Q1:} What is the true cost of agentic loops across local, hybrid, and cloud deployment architectures?

\noindent\textbf{Q2:} How do network conditions---RTT, bandwidth, radio state---affect the deployment decision?

\noindent\textbf{Q3:} Can we reduce agentic cost through thinking budgets and context compression without sacrificing accuracy?

\medskip
\noindent We make the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Pocket Agent framework.} A multi-engine (MLX, SGLang, vLLM), multi-platform measurement framework that instruments complete agentic loops with network simulation, thinking budget control, and unified energy monitoring.

\item \textbf{Three-architecture comparison.} We evaluate local, hybrid, and cloud deployment across seven RTT conditions and five models, finding that compute dominance makes the deployment decision largely network-agnostic---network adds less than 4\% overhead even at 500\,ms RTT.

\item \textbf{Early-exit thinking budget.} Capping reasoning tokens at 1024--2048 improves accuracy by 6--10 percentage points and reduces energy by 39--84\%, establishing a Pareto frontier for thinking investment.

\item \textbf{Agentic traffic characterization.} We identify agentic inference as a new mobile traffic class with median 10.2\,s inter-request gaps, where 95\% of radio energy is wasted on tail states and RRC timer tuning cannot address the structural mismatch.

\item \textbf{Context compression tradeoff.} We quantify a three-way tradeoff between bandwidth savings, accuracy, and prefix-cache efficiency, identifying a sweet spot at moderate compression (+4pp accuracy, $-$40\% bandwidth).

\item \textbf{Broad evaluation.} Seven models (including Qwen~3.5 and Gemma~4 for generalizability), four benchmarks (MBPP, HumanEval, BFCL, Web Search QA), and two hardware platforms (MacBook M2 Max, NVIDIA A100).
\end{enumerate}

Pocket Agent is released at \url{https://github.com/[anonymized]}, including all code, configurations, and analysis scripts.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{LLM Inference Systems and Runtimes.}
On-device inference engines span hand-tuned libraries such as \texttt{llama.cpp}~\cite{llamacpp} and MLX for Apple Silicon, and compiler-based frameworks like MLC-LLM~\cite{mlc-llm} that prioritize hardware portability.
Server-side systems including SGLang and vLLM exploit continuous batching, PagedAttention, and RadixAttention to maximize GPU utilization.
Recent works exploit activation locality (PowerInfer-2~\cite{powerinfer2}) or target mobile NPUs directly~\cite{llm-npu2024}.
All prior systems evaluate single-pass prompt $\rightarrow$ response generation rather than the iterative tool-calling workflows that characterize modern agents.

\textbf{Model Compression.}
Weight-only post-training quantization remains the dominant approach for deploying multi-billion parameter models on memory-constrained devices.
AWQ~\cite{awq2023} preserves quality at 4-bit precision, the GGUF format~\cite{gguf2024} standardizes block-wise quantization, and MobileQuant~\cite{mobilequant2025} pushes activation precision lower for edge deployment.
We previously showed that quantization benefits are architecture-dependent: models without native INT4 GPU kernels can regress 40$\times$ in throughput on Apple Silicon.

\textbf{LLM Offloading and Split Inference.}
DRAGON~\cite{dragon2025} proposes disaggregated inference for resource-constrained devices at MobiHoc'25, partitioning prefill and decode across edge and cloud.
Splitwise~\cite{splitwise2024} separates prefill and decode phases across GPU clusters.
EdgeShard~\cite{edgeshard2024} partitions models across heterogeneous edge devices via collaborative inference.
These systems target single-request latency; we complement them by characterizing multi-turn agentic workloads where context growth and tool-calling patterns create distinct traffic and energy profiles.

\textbf{Efficient Reasoning.}
Chain-of-thought models devote the vast majority of output tokens to reasoning.
Budget forcing~\cite{budgetforcing2025} constrains thinking length during training;
NOWAIT~\cite{nowait2025} proposes speculative execution of reasoning steps;
``Don't Overthink It''~\cite{dontoverthink2025} shows that shorter traces often yield better results.
We provide empirical evidence for this in the deployment context: our early-exit mechanism improves both accuracy and energy efficiency by terminating unproductive deliberation.

\textbf{Measurement and Characterization.}
MLPerf Inference~\cite{mlperf-inference2025} formalizes latency targets for single-pass inference.
Mobile profiling studies~\cite{xiao2025understandinglargelanguagemodels,llm-npu2024} reveal that mobile GPUs operate at under 20\% utilization and prefill dominates long-context latency.
Unlike prior work that measures prompt $\rightarrow$ response in isolation, Pocket Agent quantifies complete agentic loops across deployment architectures, including network and radio costs.

\section{Preliminaries and Metrics}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

\subsection{Agentic Execution Modes}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Execution modes for measuring LLM agents.}
\label{tab:modes}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{} l l l @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{Tools Available} & \textbf{Purpose} \\
\midrule
Base & None & Baseline: direct submission \\
Tool-Submission & Submission tool only & Structured output overhead \\
Full-Tool & Model-selected tools & Complete agentic workflow \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We evaluate agents in three modes (Table~\ref{tab:modes}).
\emph{Base} mode measures intrinsic capability via direct code generation.
\emph{Tool-Submission} mode requires structured output via a submission tool~\cite{openai2023functioncalling}, isolating the overhead of structured generation.
\emph{Full-Tool} mode enables iterative tool invocation (code execution, file operations), capturing the ReAct~\cite{yao2022react} pattern of multi-turn Tool $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Observation loops.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

\textbf{Latency.}
TTFT (time-to-first-token) captures prefill overhead; TPS (tokens/second) measures decode throughput; End-to-End Time includes all tool execution and subsequent LLM calls.

\textbf{Success.}
Pass@k is the probability a correct solution appears within $k$ attempts~\cite{chen2021evaluating}. We report Pass@1 with temperature 0.7.

\textbf{Energy.}
Energy per Token = $\mathrm{Power}_{\mathrm{gen}} / \mathrm{TPS}$.
\emph{Energy per Successful Solution} sums energy across all attempts until success:
$E_{\text{succ}} = \sum_{a=1}^{k^*} E_a$,
where $k^*$ is the first successful attempt~\cite{schwartz2020greenai}.
This deployment-focused metric captures the true cost of retries.

\subsection{Deployment Architectures}

We evaluate three deployment architectures:

\textbf{Local.} All inference runs on the device (MacBook M2 Max via MLX, or A100 via SGLang). No network traffic; latency is bounded by device throughput.

\textbf{Cloud.} All inference is offloaded to a remote server. Latency includes RTT for each request-response pair plus server-side inference time; radio energy is consumed for every tool-calling round-trip.

\textbf{Hybrid.} The device runs prefill locally and offloads decode to the cloud, or selectively offloads based on model size and network conditions, balancing local compute constraints with cloud throughput.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/pocket-agent-horiz.pdf}

  \label{fig:architecture}
  \Description{System architecture showing the Pocket Agent orchestrator managing inference engines (MLX, SGLang, vLLM), tool executor, network simulator, and monitoring components across local, hybrid, and cloud deployment paths.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Agentic Loop}

Unlike single-shot inference, agentic execution follows an iterative loop~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine}.
The model receives a task and generates a response that may include tool invocations (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{1}).
The system executes the requested tools (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{2}) and returns observations (e.g., test results or error messages).
The model processes this feedback (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{3}) and may issue further tool calls (\circledtext[height=1.9ex,charshrink=0.65]{4}) or submit a final solution.
Context grows with each iteration: memory pressure accumulates, and for cloud or hybrid deployments each round-trip incurs network latency and radio energy costs.

\subsection{Multi-Engine Support}

To evaluate deployment architectures fairly, Pocket Agent supports three inference backends:

\textbf{MLX} targets Apple Silicon (MacBook M2 Max in our evaluation). MLX exploits unified memory and Metal GPU kernels for efficient on-device inference with FP16 and INT4 precision.

\textbf{SGLang} targets NVIDIA GPUs (A100 in our evaluation). SGLang uses RadixAttention for automatic prefix caching, continuous batching, and optimized CUDA kernels. We use SGLang for all cloud-architecture experiments.

\textbf{vLLM} provides an alternative server backend with PagedAttention. We use vLLM for Gemma~4, which requires its custom attention implementation.

All engines expose a unified API for the agentic loop, enabling controlled comparisons across architectures.

\subsection{Network Simulation Layer}

To evaluate hybrid and cloud deployments under controlled conditions, Pocket Agent includes a network simulation layer with seven RTT presets: local (0\,ms), WiFi-fast (5\,ms), WiFi (20\,ms), 5G (50\,ms), 4G (100\,ms), congested (200\,ms), and satellite (500\,ms).

The simulator wraps each API call with calibrated delay injection and bandwidth throttling.
For radio energy estimation, we implement a 3GPP-based RRC state machine~\cite{huang2012close_look_power} that models Connected $\rightarrow$ Short DRX $\rightarrow$ Long DRX $\rightarrow$ Idle transitions with configurable inactivity timers (default: 7\,s tail timer, following typical LTE/5G operator configurations).
The hybrid wrapper additionally models local prefill followed by cloud decode, with network overhead applied only to the offloaded portion.

\subsection{Thinking Budget Mechanism}

Chain-of-thought models emit reasoning tokens enclosed in \texttt{<think>} tags before producing actionable output.
We show that thinking-token volume correlates \emph{negatively} with task success: the model thinks longer because it is stuck, not because extended reasoning helps (Section~\ref{sec:thinking}).

Pocket Agent implements an early-exit mechanism via two-phase generation.
In the first phase, the model generates normally until either the \texttt{</think>} tag appears or a configurable token budget $B$ is reached.
If the budget is exceeded, the system injects a \texttt{</think>} token into the context and begins the second phase, which generates only actionable output.
This mechanism requires no model modification---it operates at the inference-engine level by manipulating the token stream.

\subsection{Monitoring}

Pocket Agent records per-token timestamps, power consumption (via \texttt{powermetrics} on macOS, NVML on NVIDIA GPUs), thermal state, memory utilization, and network metrics (payload sizes, RTTs, radio state transitions).
All measurements are logged at 10\,Hz and synchronized via a unified timeline.

\subsection{Protocol}

All experiments use temperature 0.7, top-$p$ 0.95, and Pass@1 evaluation.
A100 experiments run 10 samples per task on full datasets (500 MBPP, 164 HumanEval).
MacBook experiments run the full MBPP dataset with 10 attempts.
Network experiments use the simulation layer with controlled RTT and bandwidth.
We interpret results comparatively (between modes, models, and architectures) rather than as absolute numbers, given likely training data contamination in code benchmarks.

\section{On-Device Agent Characterization}
\label{sec:ondevice}

Before evaluating deployment architectures, we characterize agent behavior on its own terms: capability baselines, thinking-token dynamics, and the early-exit mechanism that underpins our deployment analysis.

\subsection{Capability Threshold}
\label{sec:capability}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/new/fig1_pass_at_1_comparison.pdf}
  \caption{Pass@1 across seven models, three execution modes, and two benchmarks (MBPP 500, HumanEval 164). Only sufficiently capable models benefit from tools.}
  \label{fig:pass1}
  \Description{Bar chart showing Pass@1 accuracy for seven models in Base, Tool-Submission, and Full-Tool modes on MBPP and HumanEval. Qwen 3 4B gains substantially from tools while Llama and DeepSeek are hurt.}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:pass1} presents Pass@1 across all seven models and three execution modes on MBPP (500 problems) and HumanEval (164 problems).

\textbf{MBPP (Cloud, FP16).}
Qwen~3 (4B) exhibits a clear capability threshold: Pass@1 rises from 68\% (Base) to 79\% (Tool-Submission) to 77\% (Full-Tool), a net +9pp gain from structured tool use.
In contrast, Llama~3.2 drops from 50\% to 43\% ($-$7pp), and DeepSeek R1 from 42\% to 33\% ($-$9pp) in Full-Tool mode, generating malformed tool calls or invoking tools at inappropriate times.
Gemma~3n maintains Base-level performance in Tool-Submission (61\%) but drops sharply in Full-Tool (35\%): it can produce structured output but cannot sustain multi-turn tool use.
Qwen~3 (0.6B) degrades from 42\% (Base) to 34\% in both Tool-Submission and Full-Tool modes, consistent with insufficient capability for effective tool use.

\textbf{HumanEval (Cloud, FP16).}
The capability threshold pattern holds: Qwen~3 (4B) rises from 73\% (Base) to 80\% in both Tool-Submission and Full-Tool modes (+7pp), confirming that the threshold is dataset-independent.

\textbf{Generalizability probes.}
Qwen~3.5 (4B) achieves 70\%/78\%/86\% (Base/Tool-Submission/Full-Tool) on a 50-problem MBPP subset, demonstrating that newer models amplify the tool benefit (+16pp).
Gemma~4 (4B) shows 72\%/70\%/72\%, maintaining but not improving with tools---capable enough to avoid degradation but not to leverage iterative refinement (Figure~\ref{fig:generalization}).
On HumanEval, Qwen~3.5 reaches 76\%/85\%/91\% (+15pp), while Gemma~4 shows 79\%/59\%/57\% ($-$22pp), revealing that tool-calling proficiency varies substantially even among models of similar size.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/new/fig7_model_generalization.pdf}
  \caption{Generalizability probes: Qwen~3.5 and Gemma~4 (4B each) on 50-problem MBPP subset and full HumanEval. Qwen~3.5 confirms the tool-benefit pattern; Gemma~4 shows architecture-dependent behavior.}
  \label{fig:generalization}
  \Description{Bar chart comparing Qwen 3.5 and Gemma 4 across execution modes on MBPP subset and HumanEval.}
\end{figure}

\textbf{BFCL tool-calling proficiency.}
To isolate tool-calling ability from coding capability, we evaluate on BFCL (Figure~\ref{fig:bfcl}).
Qwen~3.5 achieves 100\% accuracy, Gemma~3n and Gemma~4 each reach 98\%, and Qwen~3 (4B) scores 93\%.
DeepSeek R1 scores only 49\%, confirming that its poor Full-Tool performance stems from tool-calling deficiency rather than reasoning weakness.
This decoupling of tool proficiency from task capability explains why some models (Gemma~3n: 98\% BFCL, 35\% MBPP Full-Tool) can parse tool schemas but cannot sustain productive multi-turn tool use.

  \label{fig:bfcl}
  \Description{Bar chart showing BFCL accuracy for five models, ranging from 49\% (DeepSeek) to 100\% (Qwen 3.5).}

\subsection{Thinking Token Analysis}
\label{sec:thinking}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/new/fig8_thinking_token_analysis.pdf}
  \caption{Thinking token analysis. (a)~Thinking tokens vs.\ task success shows negative correlation ($r=-0.71$). (b)~Token distribution: 84--92\% of output is thinking tokens.}
  \label{fig:thinking}
  \Description{Scatter plot showing negative correlation between thinking tokens and success rate, plus pie charts showing thinking token dominance.}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:thinking} quantifies thinking-token dynamics across all thinking models.
Across MBPP, we measure a Pearson correlation of $r=-0.71$ between thinking-token count and task success: longer reasoning traces predict failure, not success.
Thinking tokens constitute 84--92\% of total output across models and modes.
Successful attempts average 2{,}493 thinking tokens, while failed attempts average 6{,}887---a 2.8$\times$ difference.
The model thinks longer because it is stuck in unproductive loops, not because extended deliberation is beneficial.

This finding motivates our early-exit mechanism: if excessive thinking predicts failure, capping it should improve both efficiency and accuracy.

\subsection{Early-Exit Thinking Budget}
\label{sec:earlyexit}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/new/fig2_early_exit_thinking_budget.pdf}
  \caption{Early-exit thinking budget across four thinking models (MBPP, FP16). Each panel shows Pass@1 and energy as a function of thinking budget. Vertical dashed lines mark optimal budgets. Budget=2048 improves Qwen~3 (4B) by +6pp and DeepSeek R1 by +8pp while cutting energy by 39\% and 84\% respectively.}
  \label{fig:earlyexit}
  \Description{Four-panel figure showing Pass@1 and energy consumption as a function of thinking budget for Qwen 3 4B, DeepSeek R1, Qwen 3.5, and Qwen 0.6B. Optimal budgets are marked.}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:earlyexit} evaluates the early-exit mechanism across four thinking models and seven budget levels (256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192, unlimited) on MBPP.

\textbf{Qwen~3 (4B).}
At budget $B=2048$, Pass@1 reaches 78\%, a +6pp improvement over unlimited thinking (72\%).
Energy drops from 1{,}688\,J to 1{,}027\,J ($-$39\%).
Capping prevents the model from entering unproductive reasoning loops that consume thousands of tokens without progress.
Below $B=1024$, accuracy degrades as the model lacks sufficient reasoning depth for complex problems.

\textbf{DeepSeek R1 (1.5B).}
The gains are even more pronounced: $B=2048$ yields 48\% Pass@1 (+8pp over unlimited at 40\%) with energy dropping from 936\,J to 154\,J ($-$84\%).
DeepSeek R1 is particularly prone to unbounded reasoning spirals, making it the largest beneficiary of the budget constraint.

\textbf{Qwen~3.5 (4B).}
This newer model is a more efficient reasoner: optimal performance (66\%, +10pp over unlimited at 56\%) is achieved at $B=1024$, half the budget required by Qwen~3.
Energy decreases from 345\,J to 283\,J ($-$18\%), a smaller relative saving because Qwen~3.5 already produces more concise reasoning traces.

\textbf{Qwen~3 (0.6B).}
Thinking budgets provide no benefit (30\% at all budgets).
The model is too weak to leverage reasoning---it fails regardless of thinking budget.

\begin{insightbox}{Early-exit thinking budgets improve both accuracy and efficiency}
For capable thinking models, capping reasoning tokens at 1024--2048 prevents unproductive deliberation, improving Pass@1 by 6--10pp and reducing energy by 39--84\%.
The optimal budget is model-dependent: more efficient reasoners (Qwen~3.5) need lower budgets.
Models below the capability threshold (0.6B) see no benefit.
\end{insightbox}

\subsection{Energy and Cost Model}
\label{sec:costmodel}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/new/fig5_cost_model_validation.pdf}
  \caption{Energy cost model validation. $E = P \times \text{tokens}/\text{TPS}$ achieves $R^2=0.89$ across models and platforms.}
  \label{fig:costmodel}
  \Description{Scatter plot of predicted vs measured energy per attempt, showing strong linear fit with R-squared 0.89.}
\end{figure}

We validate a simple energy model: $E = P \times N / \text{TPS}$, where $P$ is mean generation power, $N$ is total tokens generated, and TPS is decode throughput.
Figure~\ref{fig:costmodel} shows this model achieves $R^2=0.89$ across all model-platform combinations, confirming that token count and throughput are sufficient predictors of energy consumption.

This model reveals two deployment-relevant findings.
First, \emph{thinking models consume $3.7\times$ more energy} than non-thinking models of comparable size, because they generate $3$--$5\times$ more tokens per attempt.
Second, an \emph{inverse efficiency paradox} persists: slower platforms (MacBook vs.\ A100) consume more total energy per task despite lower instantaneous power, because lower TPS extends generation time.
Energy per successful solution is dominated by (1)~token count (thinking overhead) and (2)~first-pass success rate (retry cost).

\section{Network-Aware Deployment}
\label{sec:network}

Having established that agentic workloads are compute-dominated and that thinking budgets substantially reduce per-task cost, we now turn to the networking dimension.
This section evaluates how network conditions affect the deployment decision, characterizes the traffic patterns that agentic workloads impose on cellular networks, and develops placement policies that account for both compute and radio energy.

\subsection{Three-Architecture Comparison}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/new/fig3_architecture_comparison.pdf}
  \caption{End-to-end latency across three deployment architectures (Local/MLX, Hybrid, Cloud/SGLang) and seven RTT conditions for five models on MBPP. Hybrid or cloud is faster for all models except Gemma~3n, which benefits from MLX's architecture-specific optimizations.}
  \label{fig:threearch}
  \Description{Multi-panel figure showing end-to-end latency for five models across local, hybrid, and cloud architectures at seven RTT values from 0ms to 500ms.}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:threearch} presents end-to-end latency across three architectures and seven RTT conditions for five models on MBPP.

\textbf{Cloud and hybrid dominate for most models.}
For Qwen~3 (4B), cloud inference via SGLang completes in 12.4\,s at 0\,ms RTT versus 38.6\,s locally via MLX---a 3.1$\times$ speedup.
Even at 500\,ms RTT, cloud latency rises to only 12.9\,s (+4\%), because network overhead (a few hundred milliseconds per round-trip $\times$ 3--5 tool iterations) is dwarfed by server-side inference time.
The hybrid architecture interpolates between the two, achieving intermediate latency.

\textbf{Architecture-dependent crossover.}
Gemma~3n is the sole exception: local inference (4.1\,s) outperforms cloud (7.2\,s) at all RTT values.
MLX exploits Gemma's selective parameter activation in ways that SGLang does not, making local execution faster despite the MacBook's lower raw throughput.
The deployment decision thus depends not only on network conditions but on the match between model architecture and inference engine.

\textbf{Network overhead is negligible.}
Across all models and RTT conditions, network overhead adds less than 4\% to end-to-end latency.
The reason is structural: agentic payloads are small (2--21\,KB per request) and tool-calling rounds are few (3--5), while inference time ranges from 3 to 63 seconds.
The ratio of network-to-compute time is therefore on the order of $10^{-2}$ even under adversarial network conditions (Figure~\ref{fig:mlxvscloud}).

\subsection{Bandwidth Analysis}
\label{sec:bandwidth}

To test whether bandwidth constraints could shift the deployment decision, we sweep bandwidth from 0.5 to 100\,Mbps across three RTT conditions.

No crossover point exists at realistic bandwidths.
At 0.5\,Mbps---below typical 4G throughput---cloud latency for Qwen~3 (4B) increases by only 0.3\,s, because payload sizes are small (median 8.4\,KB per request, 14.2\,KB per response).
Even the largest payloads (21\,KB for a Full-Tool response with extensive tool output) transfer in under 0.4\,s at 0.5\,Mbps.
The compute-to-transfer ratio exceeds 100:1 for all models at all tested bandwidths.

\emph{For current model sizes and agentic workloads, bandwidth is not a deployment constraint.}
The deployment decision should instead be driven by compute availability, energy cost, and privacy requirements.

\subsection{Agentic Traffic Characterization}
\label{sec:traffic}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/new/fig4_traffic_characterization.pdf}
  \caption{Agentic traffic characterization. (a)~CDF of inter-request gaps showing median 10.2\,s. (b)~Radio energy analysis: 95\% of energy is wasted on tail states due to structural mismatch between inference timing and RRC timers.}
  \label{fig:traffic}
  \Description{Two-panel figure. Left: CDF of inter-request time gaps for thinking and non-thinking models. Right: Radio energy breakdown showing active vs tail energy, with 95\% wasted on tail states.}
\end{figure*}

For hybrid and cloud deployments, each tool-calling round-trip triggers radio state transitions.
We characterize the resulting traffic pattern using our RRC state machine model (Figure~\ref{fig:traffic}).

\textbf{Inter-request gap distribution.}
The median inter-request gap is 10.2\,s across all models, driven by server-side inference time between tool calls.
For thinking models, the median rises to 14.7\,s (reflecting longer generation times), with 95\% of gaps exceeding the typical 7\,s RRC inactivity timer.
Non-thinking models show a lower median (6.8\,s) but still 78\% of gaps exceed the timer.

\textbf{Radio energy waste.}
Because inference time between round-trips consistently exceeds the RRC inactivity timer, the radio transitions to idle after each tool call and must be re-established for the next.
The result: 95\% of radio energy is consumed by tail states---the radio remains in high-power Connected mode for the full timer duration after each small transmission, transitions down, and is immediately re-woken for the next round-trip.
The tail-to-active energy ratio reaches 57:1 for thinking models: the radio spends 57$\times$ more energy waiting than transmitting.

\textbf{RRC timer tuning cannot fix this mismatch.}
Inference time (3--63\,s) vastly exceeds any practical RRC timer setting.
Increasing the timer to match inference time would waste energy for \emph{every} mobile application, not just agentic workloads; decreasing it further would increase re-establishment overhead.
The problem requires application-layer solutions---such as local inference for thinking tokens with cloud offloading only for actionable output---rather than radio-layer tuning.

\begin{insightbox}{Agentic workloads define a new mobile traffic class}
Unlike web browsing (sub-second gaps) or video streaming (continuous flow), agentic inference produces bursty, low-volume traffic with multi-second inter-request gaps that systematically exceed RRC timers.
This wastes 95\% of radio energy on tail states, with a 57:1 tail-to-active ratio for thinking models.
Neither RRC timer tuning nor traditional traffic shaping can address this structural mismatch.
\end{insightbox}

\subsection{Context Compression}
\label{sec:compression}

For cloud and hybrid deployments, context must be transmitted with each request.
As agentic loops progress, context grows with tool outputs, reasoning traces, and observations.
Context compression can reduce bandwidth but may degrade accuracy and---critically---invalidate server-side prefix caches.

We evaluate six compression levels (L1: none, L2: light, L3: moderate, L4: aggressive, L5: very aggressive, L6: maximum) that progressively summarize prior tool outputs, truncate thinking tokens, and collapse repeated error messages.

\textbf{Sweet spot at moderate compression.}
L3 (moderate) reduces payload size by 40\% while \emph{improving} accuracy by +4pp.
Compression removes verbose, unhelpful tool output that distracts the model, effectively acting as context curation.

\textbf{Three-way tradeoff.}
Beyond moderate compression, a three-way tradeoff emerges:
(1)~\emph{Bandwidth} decreases monotonically with compression level.
(2)~\emph{Accuracy} peaks at L3, then degrades as essential context is lost ($-$13pp at L4--L5 combined).
(3)~\emph{Prefix-cache hit rate} drops sharply with any compression, because modified context invalidates cached prefixes on the server.
At L5, prefix-cache misses increase latency by 38\%, partially offsetting bandwidth savings.
At L6, accuracy drops 12pp below baseline and degenerate iteration behavior emerges: the model re-requests information that was compressed away, increasing total latency by 138\%.

\textbf{Practical recommendation.}
L3 is the clear operating point for cloud deployments: it saves 40\% bandwidth, improves accuracy, and preserves most prefix-cache hits.
Deployments without prefix caching can push to L4 for additional bandwidth savings with minimal accuracy loss.

\subsection{Placement Policy}
\label{sec:placement}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/new/fig6_placement_policy.pdf}
  \caption{Six placement policies evaluated on MBPP across models and network conditions. COST\_AWARE achieves 97.5\% of oracle performance with 62\% less energy than always-local.}
  \label{fig:placement}
  \Description{Bar chart comparing six placement policies (always-local, always-cloud, bandwidth-aware, latency-aware, cost-aware, oracle) on combined accuracy-energy metric.}
\end{figure}

Given the architectural comparison and traffic characterization, we evaluate six placement policies (Figure~\ref{fig:placement}):

\textbf{Always-Local:} all inference on device. No network cost, but constrained by device throughput.

\textbf{Always-Cloud:} all inference offloaded. Best throughput but incurs radio energy and privacy exposure.

\textbf{Bandwidth-Aware:} offloads when estimated bandwidth exceeds a threshold. Ineffective because bandwidth is never the bottleneck (Section~\ref{sec:bandwidth}).

\textbf{Latency-Aware:} offloads when estimated RTT is below a threshold. Marginally better than always-cloud but ignores energy.

\textbf{Cost-Aware:} offloads when cloud-energy (inference + radio) is less than local-energy, using a simple two-signal rule based on (1)~the TPS ratio between cloud and local engines and (2)~model size (which determines radio energy per round-trip via payload size).

\textbf{Oracle:} selects the optimal architecture per task with perfect knowledge. Upper bound.

\textbf{Results.}
Cost-Aware achieves 97.5\% of oracle performance on a combined accuracy-energy metric, while reducing energy by 62\% versus always-local and 23\% versus always-cloud.
Concretely, it offloads to the cloud when cloud TPS exceeds local TPS by more than $2\times$ and model size exceeds 1B parameters; otherwise, it runs locally.
Gemma~3n is correctly routed locally (due to MLX's architecture advantage), while all other models are offloaded.

The bandwidth-aware policy performs equivalently to always-cloud, confirming that bandwidth is not a useful placement signal for current agentic workloads.

\subsection{Web Search QA}

To validate our findings beyond code generation, we evaluate on Web Search QA (Figure~\ref{fig:websearch}), where agents invoke a web-search tool to answer factual questions.
Unlike code-generation benchmarks, tool augmentation provides consistent benefit across all models---even weaker models gain, because the tool supplies external knowledge rather than requiring multi-turn reasoning.
The capability threshold for tool benefit is thus task-dependent: retrieval tasks have a lower threshold than iterative code refinement.

The traffic pattern differs from code generation: inter-request gaps are shorter (median 4.1\,s) because web-search responses are smaller, reducing inference time.
However, 68\% of gaps still exceed the 7\,s RRC timer for thinking models, confirming that radio energy waste persists across task types.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our measurements span seven models, four benchmarks, and seven network conditions. We now revisit the three research questions posed in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}.

\textbf{Q1: What is the true cost of agentic loops across deployment architectures?}
Agentic workloads are compute-dominated. Cloud deployment via SGLang provides 2--3$\times$ lower latency than local MLX inference for most models, and network overhead adds less than 4\% even at 500\,ms RTT.
The exception is Gemma~3n, where architecture-specific local optimizations outperform the cloud.
Thinking models consume 3.7$\times$ more energy than non-thinking models of comparable size, driven by token volume rather than per-token cost.

\textbf{Q2: How do network conditions affect the deployment decision?}
Network conditions have minimal impact on latency (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}) and bandwidth is never the bottleneck (Section~\ref{sec:bandwidth}).
The primary network cost is \emph{energetic}: agentic traffic produces multi-second inter-request gaps that systematically exceed RRC timers, wasting 95\% of radio energy on tail states (Section~\ref{sec:traffic}).
A simple cost-aware placement policy that accounts for both compute and radio energy achieves 97.5\% of oracle performance (Section~\ref{sec:placement}).

\textbf{Q3: Can we reduce cost through thinking budgets and context compression?}
Yes. Early-exit thinking budgets at 1024--2048 tokens improve accuracy by 6--10pp and reduce energy by 39--84\% for capable thinking models (Section~\ref{sec:earlyexit}).
Moderate context compression saves 40\% bandwidth while improving accuracy by 4pp (Section~\ref{sec:compression}).
The two mechanisms are complementary: thinking budgets reduce compute cost, while compression reduces network payload without sacrificing prefix-cache efficiency.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Systems guidelines.}
Based on our findings, we distill guidelines across three dimensions:

\emph{Deployment.}
For models above 1B parameters without architecture-specific local advantages, cloud offloading is preferred because compute savings dominate network costs.
The cost-aware placement rule (TPS ratio + model size) suffices; bandwidth monitoring adds no value.

\emph{Inference.}
Thinking budgets should be enabled by default for thinking models, with $B{=}2048$ as a conservative starting point and $B{=}1024$ for efficient reasoners.
Context compression at L3 (moderate) is the default operating point for cloud deployments.

\emph{Networking.}
Agentic workloads represent a new traffic class poorly served by current cellular radio resource management.
Application-layer solutions---batching tool outputs, local reasoning with cloud-offloaded action, or predictive radio wake-up---are needed to address the structural mismatch between inference timing and RRC timers.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Limitations.}
Our evaluation covers code-generation and question-answering tasks; extending to multi-modal agents and longer-horizon planning would broaden applicability.
Network conditions are simulated rather than measured on real cellular networks.
Mobile phone evaluation (performed in our prior work on iPhone and Android) is excluded from the architecture comparison because SGLang and vLLM lack mobile ports.
The RRC energy model uses standard 3GPP parameters; operator-specific configurations may differ.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Future work.}
Three directions follow from our findings:
(1)~deploying the hybrid architecture on real mobile devices over cellular networks to validate the simulated traffic characterization;
(2)~extending to multi-agent scenarios where models coordinate across devices, multiplying both compute and network costs;
(3)~co-designing radio resource management policies with application-layer inference scheduling to eliminate the 95\% radio energy waste identified in this study.

\begin{insightbox}{Compute dominance simplifies deployment}
For current agentic workloads with models up to 4B parameters, the deployment decision is driven by compute availability and energy cost, not network conditions.
A two-signal placement rule (TPS ratio, model size) achieves near-oracle performance.
The networking challenge is not latency or bandwidth but radio energy waste from timing mismatches between inference patterns and cellular RRC state machines.
\end{insightbox}
